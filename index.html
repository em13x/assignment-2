Overview

The internet and other types of communication systems are things that we use on the daily, and yet we never really think about how they work nor their presence in the physical world. To make these systems and infrastructures work, generally mass cable systems are needed to send information from one place to another. These cables don’t just link a country together; they link countries to other countries, continents to other continents. These submarine cables are so vital to today’s society, and most people who use them don’t even know they exist, or don’t care about their existence until something goes wrong.

That’s where ORB comes in.

ORB is a conceptual phone application in which users can discover and monitor the status of submarine and subterranean cables around the world. This would serve a multitude of purposes, from internet and electrical infrastructure education for students, to a globalised monitoring tool for internet and electricity providers. It would even allow for people who are experiencing internet blackouts or lag to discover exactly where and why the situation is occurring, if the problem is due to the monitored cables.

How it works:

Test it for yourself!

Beginning at the landing page, users have the option to either regsiter or login to access the app. There's also a "help" button below to give instructions on how the app works. However, for the purposes of this prototype, users can directly click the "register" or "login" buttons at the bottom of their respective screens, or by simply clicking the logo on the intial landing page, to access the app, without putting in any personal details.

The main home screen features the interactive globe. As this is a prototype, the Earth isn't maneuverable as it would be in the real app, but is instead static to simply give users a feel for how the main screen would look and work. Users can choose different points on the globe to learn more about specific submarine cables. This brings up a new screen with the desired information. Users can click the "back" button at the bottom of the screen to return to the main home screen.

In the top right corner, the simplified logo acts at the menu button. Selecting it brings up a new screen with a menu list that users can use to navigate the app.

The "News" page displays the latest news from around the internet to do with the topic of submarine cables. In the full version of the app, users would be able to simply click on one of the thumbnails and be taken to the corresponding news webpage.

The "Random" function randomly chooses a submarine cable and presents it to the user; a great function for discovering new and interesting cables!

The "FAQ" page is the same as the "Help" page accessible from the landing page, except with the option to go back to the main menu instead of the landing page.

Lastly, the "Log Out" function brings the user to a farewell screen, before directing them back to the landing page.

Overall, the orb app prototype is one that is simple yet effective; easy to navigate, in an organic workflow that should make sense to any tech user.

Conclusion

Overall, I think the prototype came out as well as I could make it. Originally my idea was very different, and while I think the animation idea might have been better in demonstrating what the app would really feel and work like, it wouldn't be interactive, which is arguably the most important aspect of a prototype. With the prototype made in Invision Studios, users can really test for themselves what the app would act and feel like, and I'm happy with the outcome (even if it isn't exactly what I originally intended).
